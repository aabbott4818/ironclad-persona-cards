Teaching narrative

One message, six entry points — now with the hidden flaw made explicit.

Accepted truth

Legal is responsible for managing contract risk.

Hidden flaw

Legal's performance is judged on speed, but the riskiest decisions move fastest — which means the highest-stakes work consistently gets the least senior attention.

Reframe

New way

CLM with AI does not replace legal judgment — it removes repetitive work, improves visibility, and makes that judgment more scalable.

Quantifying the value of Ironclad

Earn the evaluation by building the model with the buyer.

Within the first 30 minutes, a buyer will ask “what’s the ROI?”. Ironclad does not publish a unified per-customer ROI figure, and that’s actually a strength — it lets you build the value model with the buyer using their own numbers, which is what their CFO will fund. Five lenses, in order of defensibility per persona.

Be honest first. The cohort signal Ironclad does publish is the State of Contract Management 2025 work and named customer outcomes (Hormel 75% cycle reduction, Skillsoft 15 days→3, NEXT Insurance 50% legal-ops time back). Those are evidence, not a model. Don’t quote a synthetic ROI. Use the five lenses below to build a model the buyer will defend internally.
Lens 1 · GC, Legal Ops

Legal capacity recovered

“Ironclad’s value shows up where senior lawyers spend hours on work that doesn’t need their judgment.”

Contracts/yr × % low-judgment × review hrs × loaded cost − Ironclad cost

Anchors: Gartner — ~3.2 hours per contract review at $400–900 each. WorldCC 2023 (n=759) — 26–30% of the workforce touches contracts. NEXT Insurance recovered 50% of legal-ops time after Jurist + Workflow Designer adoption.

Discovery ask: “Of the contracts your team touches each year, what share genuinely need senior judgment — and what share are repeat patterns your most expensive lawyers shouldn’t be reviewing?”

Lens 2 · Revenue, CRO

Revenue velocity recovered

“Standard contracts shouldn’t need a lawyer. Reps shouldn’t chase signatures. Deals shouldn’t die in legal review.”

Deals/yr × % slipping in contracting × avg deal value × conversion uplift

Anchors: Sales contracts average 35 days to signature (Ironclad 2026). Ironclad reports 70% of its own commercial deals close without legal review. Skillsoft moved legal cycle from 15 days to 3. Demostack cut NDA cycle by 90%.

Discovery ask: “What share of your forecast is currently sitting in legal or contracting at quarter-end — and what would your forecast accuracy look like if that bottleneck wasn’t the determining variable?”

Lens 3 · CFO, Finance

Value leakage prevented

“Companies don’t lose money negotiating bad contracts. They lose money failing to enforce the good ones they signed.”

Annual contract value × leakage % recovered − Ironclad cost

Anchors: WorldCC — 8.6–9.2% of annual contract value lost to poor post-signature management. WorldCC 2023 (n=759) bid-to-signature: 4.4 wks (low) / 11.6 wks (medium) / 24 wks (high). Hormel 75% cycle reduction; Gusto 6× volume on flat headcount.

Lens 4 · Finance, GC

Audit and obligation exposure

General Counsel

Strategic legal leader under volume pressure

Risk allocationDefensibilityBoard visibility
Person / world

The GC is a strategic advisor to the CEO and board who wants legal seen as a business enabler, not a contract-processing centre. They live in escalations, board risk reporting, and the question "are we exposed?" — and they are judged on outcomes that rarely make the news but can end careers when they do. Their team is handling more matters with the same or fewer people: 81% of in-house departments report rising volumes while 56% say they're under-resourced. They speak in risk allocation, defensibility, precedent, and "who signed off." What they fear most is being reactive — a contract-processing function instead of the strategic voice in the room.

Problem statement

When contract volume outruns team capacity, senior legal judgment gets spent on low-leverage work, the riskiest decisions get the least senior attention, and the GC slides from strategic advisor to bottleneck — exactly the perception they're trying to escape.

Hidden flaw
Accepted truth

Legal must touch every contract to protect the company.

Hidden flaw

Touching everything means judging by speed — so the standard, low-risk work crowds out the high-stakes review, and the most dangerous clauses get the least senior eyes.

Reframe

The question isn't "how does legal review more, faster?" but "how do we route judgment to only the work that actually carries risk?"

New way

AI-assisted CLM removes the repetitive first pass and surfaces exceptions — so senior counsel spends time where the exposure actually lives.

~30%of the workforce touches contract management — it's an enterprise risk, not a legal-only one (WorldCC/Icertis 2023).
46%of dealmakers cite due-diligence gaps as the top driver of post-close M&A disputes — a GC's reputational tail risk (BRG 2026).

IT / CISO

Security gatekeeper who owns third-party liability

Third-party riskDPAsAudit rights
Person / world

The CISO's title says "Chief Information Security Officer" but the job is really "Chief Third-Party Liability Officer." Every vendor contract is a potential data-breach vector, and regulators, cyber insurers, and boards have made clear that "we didn't know" is no longer a defence. They report to the CEO or CTO and spend their days triaging threat intel, defending budget to the CFO, and fielding questions they can't fully answer — like "which of our vendors has access to customer PII right now?" They're measured on audit outcomes, breach count, and board reporting under SEC disclosure rules, NIS2, and DORA. What they fear is the quiet breach that started at a third party three months ago, inside a contract no one thought was their problem. They speak in attack surface, data flows, sub-processors, residency, and incident-notification windows.

Problem statement

Every uncatalogued contract is an uncatalogued data flow. The CISO can only govern vendors they know about, but contracts get signed across Legal, Procurement, Finance, and the business — often without security review — so when the board asks "which third parties touch regulated data?", the honest answer is "we think we know, but we can't prove it." That gap between belief and demonstrable evidence is where the risk actually lives.

Hidden flaw
Accepted truth

We have a vendor risk process — every new vendor goes through security review before approval.

Hidden flaw

The review happens once, at onboarding — but the contract lives 3–7 years. Sub-processors swap, DPAs go stale, a vendor adds AI model-training as a default. The review is a point-in-time snapshot on a living relationship, and the contract that defines audit rights and breach SLAs sits as an unsearchable PDF that IT can't query.

Reframe

Vendor risk isn't only a security-posture question; it's a contract-governance question. A security review without a searchable contract repository is a policy without enforcement.

New way

CLM becomes part of the TPRM stack: query the repository for every vendor with a DPA, every contract missing a 72-hour breach clause, every unspecified residency — risk management goes from reactive to systematic.

Procurement / CPO

Savings owner judged on value that must actually materialise

Person / world

The CPO is asked to do more with fewer people, under more cost pressure, against a more volatile supply chain, while running a digital transformation. They report to the CFO or COO and are measured on hard targets: cost savings, cost avoidance, supplier performance, and spend under management. They spend their days in savings reviews with finance, cycle-time escalations with legal, and meetings with business leaders who went around procurement because "legal took too long." Their fear is irrelevance — being routed around by maverick spend, second-guessed by finance on whether savings ever materialised, or automated away. They speak in spend under management, savings realisation, supplier performance, value leakage, and maverick buying.

Problem statement

Procurement's credibility rests on two claims — "we got a good deal" and "the deal delivered what we negotiated." The second is where money disappears: an average 11% of contract value leaks post-signature through missed savings, unmanaged price changes, and dormant obligations. The post-award world is owned by nobody in particular, and procurement carries the blame when negotiated value never reaches the P&L.

Hidden flaw
Accepted truth

We track savings at the point of negotiation — a 15% discount goes into the savings tracker and we report it to the CFO.

Hidden flaw

The savings tracker is a negotiation ledger, not a realisation ledger. Actual spend flows through AP to whatever vendor the business unit used — maverick spend costs 10–20% of targeted savings and can account for up to 80% of invoices, so the "savings" were never enforced.

Reframe

Procurement's reporting problem is a contract-visibility problem: savings claims are unauditable when no one can answer "how much of our spend is actually on a signed, current contract?"

New way

CLM shifts procurement from "we negotiated X" to "we realised X" — obligations, pricing triggers, and renewal terms become live data, closing the gap between negotiated intent and operational reality.

Commercial consequence
11%of contracted spend leaks post-award (15%+ in complex ecosystems) — ~$43–55M/yr on a $500M book (WorldCC/Ironclad 2026).
91%of procurement leaders call maverick spend a challenge; 87% say it grew year over year (ProcureCon/SDI 2024).

Consensus Map

By Tension

Five pains that no single seat owns. Use these to connect the room: the bridge sentence links the personas, and the committee question surfaces the tension out loud.

1 Speed vs. control

RevenueLegal / GCProcurement

Revenue wants velocity, Legal wants control, and Procurement wants standardisation — and today each one's win feels like another's loss, because there's no agreed line between the deals that need judgment and the deals that just need a path.

The reframe that dissolves it: speed and control aren't opposites once the lifecycle routes legal judgment only to the risk-bearing minority. Ironclad's own book runs 70% of commercial deals with no legal review — control held, velocity gained.

Surfaces it in a committee setting

"If we could all agree on which contracts genuinely need legal judgment versus which just need a safe, fast path — what percentage of your volume falls in each bucket? And who here disagrees with that line?"

2 Visibility gaps

GCFinanceIT / CISO

The GC is blind to where risk-bearing work is moving, Finance is blind to obligations buried in PDFs, and the CISO is blind to which vendor contracts touch regulated data — three different blind spots, one root cause: contract data that can't be queried.

Connecting evidence: 71% of companies can't locate 10%+ of their contracts, and that data sits across 24 systems. A searchable Repository is the shared answer to three separate "we think so" problems.

Surfaces it in a committee setting

"If the board asked all three of you — GC, CFO, CISO — the same question tomorrow: 'show me our exposure,' how long would each of you take to answer, and would your answers even agree?"

3 Capacity / scale

Legal OpsRevenueProcurement

Legal Ops, Revenue, and Procurement are all absorbing volume their headcount can't — more matters, more deals, more vendors — and all three are being told to do it without adding people.

Connecting evidence: 81% of legal departments report rising volume while 56% are under-resourced; the same structural squeeze hits sales reps (23 days/yr lost to non-negotiated paper) and procurement (20–40K active contracts). The unlock is the same: remove repetitive work so the same team absorbs more.

Surfaces it in a committee setting

"Each of you has a volume target that grew faster than your headcount. Where is the repetitive, low-judgment work that — if it disappeared — would give your team the capacity you're being asked to find?"

4 Audit & accountability

FinanceIT / CISOProcurement

Finance, the CISO, and Procurement all carry post-signature risk exposure that nobody formally owns — SOX obligations, stale DPAs, unenforced supplier terms — and each is the one in the room when an auditor or a breach asks "what does the contract say?"

Connecting evidence: third-party breaches average $4.91M and 267 days to resolve, inadvertent auto-renewals cost $50–250K each, and 11% of spend leaks post-award. The shared fix is post-signature governance: obligations and terms as live, owned, alertable data.

Surfaces it in a committee setting

"After a contract is signed, who in this room actually owns making sure its terms are enforced — the renewals, the breach SLAs, the savings? If the answer is 'everyone and no one,' that's the gap we should talk about."
